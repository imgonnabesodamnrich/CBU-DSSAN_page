Paper Note: Highly Imbalanced Fault Diagnosis of Gas Turbines via CBU-DSSAN

A Fault Diagnosis Framework for Gas Turbines under Highly Imbalanced Data

Paper Title: Highly imbalanced fault diagnosis of gas turbines via clustering-based downsampling and deep siamese self-attention network

Journal: Advanced Engineering Informatics, Vol. 54, 2022

DOI: 10.1016/j.aei.2022.101725

1. Research Background

Gas turbines are core equipment in aviation and industrial fields. In actual operation, due to their extremely high reliability, the vast majority of historical monitoring data consists of normal state data, while fault data is extremely scarce. This data distribution constitutes a typical "Highly Imbalanced Data" scenario.

Under imbalanced data, traditional deep learning classification models tend to focus more on the majority class (normal samples). Because the model can achieve a high overall accuracy even if it predicts all inputs as normal, this leads to a sharp rise in the missed diagnosis rate of fault samples.

Furthermore, minor fault samples often highly overlap with normal samples in the feature space (Class Overlapping), making it difficult for conventional classification boundaries to distinguish them effectively. Traditional time-series models also face the problem of insufficient temporal correlation extraction when processing multi-dimensional monitoring data. Therefore, the paper proposes a diagnosis framework combining data-layer and model-layer optimization — CBU-DSSAN.

2. CBU-DSSAN Method Architecture

Fig 2. Overall flowchart of the CBU-DSSAN fault diagnosis framework

The method consists of two main technologies: Clustering-Based Downsampling (CBU) for balancing data, and Deep Siamese Self-Attention Network (DSSAN) for feature extraction and classification.

2.1 Data Layer: Clustering-based Downsampling (CBU)

The most intuitive way to deal with data imbalance is to downsample the majority class. However, traditional random downsampling easily loses key representative information. The paper adopts a downsampling strategy based on K-means clustering:

  1. Perform cluster analysis only on the massive normal samples, dividing them into multiple clusters.
  2. Extract the center point (Cluster Center) of each cluster as a representative of the normal samples.
  3. Combine these representative normal samples with the rare fault samples to form a new, class-balanced training set.

This approach reduces the absolute number of normal samples while preserving the core data distribution characteristics of the normal state.

2.2 Model Layer: Deep Siamese Self-Attention Network (DSSAN)

After obtaining a balanced dataset, the model needs to solve the problems of sample overlap and feature extraction. The DSSAN module contains two core mechanisms:

1. Siamese Network Architecture
Unlike traditional models that classify a single sample directly, the Siamese network receives "sample pairs" as input. The training goal is to judge whether the pair belongs to the same category. By introducing a Contrastive Loss function, the model pulls samples of the same class closer in the feature space and pushes samples of different classes further apart during training. This mechanism effectively increases the distance between normal and fault samples in the feature space, thereby mitigating the misjudgment problem caused by class overlapping.

2. Self-Attention Mechanism
Gas turbine monitoring data is typical multi-dimensional time series. As the feature extraction module of the Siamese network, the Self-Attention Network (SANet) can focus globally on data points at different positions and dimensions simultaneously. Through the Multi-head Self-Attention mechanism, the model can more comprehensively capture dependencies and evolution laws of data in the temporal dimension.

3. Experiment and Data Processing

The study used real flight operation data from an Asian airline. Considering that directly measured parameters are disturbed by the environment and thrust, the experiment selected baseline deviation values converted by the Original Equipment Manufacturer (OEM) as feature parameters, mainly including:

  • Exhaust Gas Temperature Margin (EGTM)
  • Exhaust Gas Temperature Deviation (DEGT)
  • Core Speed Deviation (DN2)
  • Fuel Flow Deviation (DFF)

For time-series characteristics, a sliding window method was used to construct samples, and five-fold cross-validation was used to eliminate the randomness of a single experiment. The dataset included 984 normal samples, along with a small number of Exhaust Temperature Indication (EIF) and Total Air Temperature Sensor Indication (TSIF) fault samples.

4. Conclusion

By comparing mainstream deep networks (such as ConvNet, RNN, LSTM, GRU) and conventional resampling methods (SMOTE oversampling, ordinary clustering downsampling), the experiment obtained objective performance data:

  1. Accuracy Improvement: In the Balanced Accuracy metric, CBU-DSSAN reached 92.26%, significantly better than standalone baseline models and control groups combined with other sampling strategies.
  2. Feature Separation Effect: 2D feature visualization (t-SNE) showed that in the feature space extracted by traditional models, fault samples were heavily mixed with normal samples. In contrast, in the space extracted by CBU-DSSAN, different categories of samples showed clear boundaries, verifying the effectiveness of the Siamese network's contrastive loss.
  3. Inference Efficiency: Although the Siamese network's structure is complex, after training, the inference time for a single test sample is about 1.33 milliseconds, fully meeting real-time monitoring requirements in industrial scenarios.

This method demonstrates that combining data-level data compression (preserving representativeness) with algorithm-level distance metric learning (expanding inter-class distance) is a feasible path when handling extremely imbalanced industrial fault data with class overlapping.
